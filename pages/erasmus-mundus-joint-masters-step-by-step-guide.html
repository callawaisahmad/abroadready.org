Erasmus Mundus Joint Masters: A Step-by-Step Guide

🗓️ Jun 25, 2026⏱️ 12 min read✍️ AbroadReady Team

Erasmus Mundus Joint Masters are among the most attractive fully funded study opportunities in Europe. Funded by the European Union, an Erasmus Mundus programme lets you complete a single master's degree while studying in at least two different countries, taught by a consortium of top universities, and often with a generous scholarship attached. For students who want an international, multi-country experience with strong funding, few options compare.

This step-by-step guide explains what Erasmus Mundus Joint Masters are, how the scholarship works, who can apply, and how to build a strong application. The programme structure can look confusing at first, so we will break it down clearly.

Key takeaways

  • Erasmus Mundus Joint Masters (EMJMs) are integrated master's programmes delivered by a consortium of universities in at least two countries.
  • The EU scholarship is typically fully funded, covering tuition, travel and installation costs, and a monthly living allowance.
  • You apply directly to the individual programme, not to a central portal, and each has its own deadline (often in the autumn or winter).
  • Applicants from anywhere in the world can apply; there are usually a limited number of scholarships per programme.
  • You can typically apply to a limited number of Erasmus Mundus programmes in the same round, so choose carefully.

What are Erasmus Mundus Joint Masters?

An Erasmus Mundus Joint Masters is a prestigious, integrated study programme jointly designed and delivered by a group of universities, called a consortium, based in different countries. When you graduate, you receive either a joint degree or multiple degrees recognised by all the participating institutions.

The defining feature is mobility. You spend time studying at two or more universities in different countries, following a common curriculum. You might start in one country, move to another for your second semester or year, and complete your thesis at a third or back at the first. This built-in international experience is what makes Erasmus Mundus distinctive and highly valued by employers.

How the Erasmus Mundus scholarship works

The EU funds a number of scholarships for each Erasmus Mundus Joint Masters. While exact amounts are reviewed over time, the scholarship typically covers:

  • Tuition and participation costs for the whole programme.
  • A monthly living allowance for the duration of the master's.
  • A contribution to travel and installation costs, sometimes higher for students coming from further away.

Scholarships are competitive and limited in number per programme. Even if you are not awarded a full scholarship, you may still be admitted as a self-funded or partially funded student, and some consortia offer additional or regional scholarships. Erasmus Mundus eligibility

Eligibility is broad, which is part of the appeal:

  • Applicants from any country in the world can usually apply.
  • You generally need a relevant bachelor's degree (or be in your final year and able to graduate before the programme starts).
  • English proficiency is almost always required, evidenced by IELTS or TOEFL, since most programmes are taught in English.
  • Specific programmes may require particular academic backgrounds, prerequisite subjects, or minimum grades.

There are also rules to ensure fairness in scholarship allocation, such as limits on how many scholars can come from the same country, and restrictions on applying to too many programmes at once in the same intake. Check each programme's rules.

How to apply for Erasmus Mundus, step by step

1. Explore the official catalogue

The European Commission maintains an official catalogue of Erasmus Mundus Joint Masters. Browse it by subject to find programmes that match your academic interests. Each entry links to the consortium's own website, which is where you will find full details and the application portal.

2. Shortlist programmes that fit your goals

Because you can only apply to a limited number of Erasmus Mundus programmes for a scholarship in the same intake, be strategic. Compare curricula, the universities involved, the countries you would live in, the mobility pathway, and any specialisation tracks. Fit matters more than prestige alone.

3. Check requirements and deadlines carefully

Each programme sets its own deadline, commonly in the autumn or winter for study starting the following autumn. Scholarship deadlines are often earlier than self-funded deadlines. Note exactly what each consortium requires, because requirements vary.

4. Prepare your application documents

Typical documents include:

  • A motivation letter tailored to the specific programme.
  • A CV, sometimes in Europass format.
  • Academic transcripts and degree certificates, often with certified translations.
  • An English language certificate.
  • Letters of recommendation.
  • Sometimes a research statement, portfolio, or additional essays, depending on the field.

5. Write a targeted motivation letter

Your motivation letter should explain why this particular consortium, why this multi-country structure suits your goals, and how the programme fits your longer-term plans. Generic letters perform poorly. Reference the specific universities, tracks, and mobility path. Our SOP builder can help you build a focused, programme-specific statement.

6. Submit and track each application

Apply through each consortium's own portal and keep careful records of deadlines and submitted documents. Because you may apply to more than one programme, staying organised is essential.

How to strengthen your Erasmus Mundus application

Demonstrate genuine interest in mobility

Erasmus Mundus is built around studying across borders. Show that you understand and embrace this: talk about why an international, multi-country education matters for your field and your ambitions, not just the funding.

Tailor every application

Each consortium designed its programme with a purpose. Read the aims, the specialisation tracks, and the partner strengths, then reflect them in your motivation letter. This signals that you have done your research and are a serious fit.

Highlight relevant academic and practical background

Selectors look for academic readiness and, where relevant, practical experience. Connect your past coursework, projects, internships, or work to the programme's focus so your suitability is obvious.

Get your documents right

Certified translations, correctly formatted CVs, and valid language certificates all matter. Administrative errors can sink an otherwise strong application, so double-check each requirement. Before submitting, consider having our AI advisor review your motivation letter for clarity and impact.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Applying to too many or poorly matched programmes. There are limits, and scattered applications rarely succeed.
  • Using one generic motivation letter. Each consortium wants to see specific interest in their programme.
  • Missing scholarship deadlines. These are often earlier than general admission deadlines.
  • Overlooking prerequisites. Some programmes require specific academic backgrounds.
  • Underestimating document requirements. Translations and certificates take time to prepare.

Understanding the consortium and degree structure

A key concept in Erasmus Mundus is the consortium. Instead of one university running the programme, several partner institutions across different countries jointly design and deliver it. This has real advantages: you benefit from the combined expertise and networks of multiple universities, you experience different academic cultures, and your degree is recognised across all of them.

When you graduate, you receive either a single joint degree issued collectively by the consortium, or multiple (double or multiple) degrees from the individual universities. Both are fully recognised qualifications. In addition, participating in the programme is documented in a way that signals to employers you completed a demanding, EU-endorsed international master's. Understanding this structure helps you write a more convincing application, because you can speak knowledgeably about why the multi-university model appeals to you.

How the mobility pathway works

Each programme sets a defined mobility pathway. For example, you might complete your first semester at a university in one country, your second at a partner in another, and then choose a specialisation track or thesis placement at a third. Some programmes offer several optional routes so you can tailor your experience to your interests. Before applying, study the mobility structure closely: the countries, the sequence, and any choices you will make along the way. This directly affects your living costs, visa arrangements, and overall experience, and referencing it thoughtfully in your motivation letter shows genuine engagement.

The Erasmus Mundus application timeline

Because each consortium runs its own admissions, exact dates differ, but the broad pattern is consistent. Planning around it prevents missed deadlines:

  • Autumn to winter: Scholarship application deadlines for most programmes typically fall in this window for study beginning the following autumn.
  • Winter to spring: Selection and results, with scholarship candidates usually notified first.
  • Spring to summer: Visa applications, enrolment, and accommodation arrangements across your mobility countries.
  • Autumn: Your programme begins at the first university in your pathway.

Scholarship deadlines are frequently earlier than self-funded deadlines, so aim for the earliest relevant date. Start preparing documents, especially certified translations and language certificates, several months ahead.

How competitive are Erasmus Mundus scholarships?

Scholarships are limited in number for each programme and attract applicants worldwide, so they are competitive. However, admission to a programme and award of a scholarship are related but distinct outcomes. You may be admitted without a full scholarship, and some consortia offer additional or regional scholarships beyond the main EU-funded places. Applying to well-matched programmes, rather than the most famous ones regardless of fit, improves your realistic chances.

Managing life across multiple countries

Studying in two or more countries is exciting but logistically demanding. You will likely deal with more than one set of visa or residence permit rules, arrange accommodation in each location, and adapt to different academic and social cultures. The consortium usually provides support, but you should plan proactively.

Practical considerations include understanding the visa requirements for each country in your pathway, budgeting for moving between locations, and preparing for language and cultural differences even when your programme is taught in English. Embracing this mobility is part of the value of Erasmus Mundus, and demonstrating that you are ready for it strengthens your application and your experience alike.

Building your career from an Erasmus Mundus degree

Employers across sectors value the adaptability, cross-cultural communication, and international networks that Erasmus Mundus graduates develop. To make the most of this, engage actively with all the universities in your consortium, build relationships with peers from around the world, and take advantage of any internships, industry projects, or research placements the programme offers. The alumni networks associated with these programmes can be a lasting professional asset. Studying and living across Europe

An Erasmus Mundus Joint Masters is more than a funded degree; it is an immersive experience of living and studying in multiple countries, building an international network, and graduating with a degree recognised across Europe. Employers value the adaptability, language exposure, and cross-cultural skills that come with it.

Practical considerations before you commit

Beyond the academics and funding, a few practical factors deserve thought before you accept an Erasmus Mundus place. Each will shape your day-to-day experience across your mobility countries.

  • Visas and residence permits: Studying in multiple countries can mean navigating more than one immigration process. The consortium usually guides you, but build in time for paperwork.
  • Cost of living differences: The living allowance is fixed, but costs vary between countries and cities. Budget for the more expensive locations in your pathway.
  • Accommodation: You may need to arrange housing in each country. Ask the consortium what support exists and start searching early.
  • Language and integration: Even in English-taught programmes, learning some of the local language enriches your experience and helps with daily life.

Thinking these through in advance not only smooths your experience but also demonstrates, in interviews or communications with the consortium, that you are a serious and prepared candidate.

Self-funded and alternative options

If you are admitted to an Erasmus Mundus Joint Masters but do not receive a full EU scholarship, you still have options. Some consortia offer additional or partial scholarships, including awards targeted at students from specific regions. You may also be able to combine self-funding with other national or institutional scholarships. Because admission itself is a strong achievement, it is worth exploring every avenue before ruling out a programme on cost grounds. Contact the consortium directly to ask about any additional funding they administer, as these opportunities are not always widely advertised.

Is Erasmus Mundus right for you?

It is also worth remembering that the experience is demanding as well as rewarding. Moving between countries, adapting to different teaching styles, and managing logistics all require resilience. Students who thrive tend to be organised, independent, and genuinely curious about other cultures. If that describes you, the challenges become part of the appeal rather than a drawback.

Erasmus Mundus suits students who are genuinely excited by international mobility, who are academically strong, and who want a degree recognised across Europe. It asks for adaptability, organisation, and openness to new cultures. If you prefer to remain in one place for your whole degree, a single-country master's may fit better. But if the idea of studying in several countries, building a global network, and graduating with a prestigious, EU-backed qualification appeals to you, few programmes offer a comparable combination of experience and funding.

If you are drawn to an international education, a strong academic challenge, and generous EU funding, Erasmus Mundus is well worth the effort. Start early, choose programmes that genuinely fit your goals, and craft applications that show both academic strength and a real appetite for a border-crossing education.
