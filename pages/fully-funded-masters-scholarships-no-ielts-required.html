Fully Funded Masters Scholarships With No IELTS Required

🗓️ Apr 16, 2026⏱️ 10 min read✍️ AbroadReady Team

The IELTS exam is a barrier for many talented students: it costs money, requires travel to a test centre, and adds pressure to an already demanding application. The good news is that scholarships without IELTS genuinely exist, and many fully funded masters programmes will accept alternative evidence of your English or waive the requirement entirely. This guide explains how these waivers work and how to find and win scholarships without IELTS.

It is important to be honest from the start: no IELTS does not mean no English requirement. Universities still need to know you can cope with study in English. What changes is how you prove it. Once you understand the alternatives, a whole range of funded options opens up.

Key takeaways

  • No IELTS is common, not rare. Many universities accept alternative proof of English or waive the test in specific situations.
  • Waivers usually rest on one of a few grounds: prior study in English, a waiver letter, an interview, or an accepted alternative test.
  • Prepare evidence early. A medium-of-instruction letter from your previous university is one of the most powerful documents you can hold.
  • Read each programme carefully. Waiver policies vary by university and even by department.
  • Never assume. Always confirm the exact requirement in writing before you rely on a waiver.

How English requirements really work

Universities require proof of English because degrees are demanding and they want you to succeed. IELTS is simply the most common way to provide that proof, not the only way. Many institutions accept a range of evidence, and some waive formal testing when your background already demonstrates strong English.

This distinction matters. When you search for scholarships without IELTS, you are really searching for programmes that accept an alternative form of proof. Reframing the question this way makes your search far more productive.

Common ways to get an IELTS waiver

There are several established routes to studying with funding but no IELTS. Most successful applicants use one of the following.

1. Previous study in English (medium of instruction)

This is the most common and reliable route. If your previous degree was taught entirely in English, many universities will accept a medium-of-instruction letter from that institution instead of IELTS. The letter, issued on official letterhead, confirms that English was the language of teaching and assessment.

If your undergraduate degree was in English, request this letter early. It is free, it is powerful, and it unlocks a large number of programmes.

2. Studying in an English-speaking country

Some universities waive the English test if you completed your prior education in a country where English is an official language, or where you spent a qualifying period studying in English. Policies differ, so check the specific list each university publishes.

3. A formal waiver letter or interview

Certain programmes assess English through a short interview or by issuing a conditional waiver at the admissions office's discretion. In these cases the admissions team judges your English directly, often during a video call that also covers your motivation.

4. Accepted alternative tests

Even where a test is required, IELTS is often not the only one accepted. Universities may accept other recognised English tests, some of which are cheaper or can be taken online at home. If you must take a test, check which alternatives your target programmes accept before booking anything.

Which programmes are most likely to waive IELTS

Some categories of scholarship and programme are more flexible than others.

Government scholarships in non-English-speaking countries

Many national scholarship schemes in countries where English is not the first language are pragmatic about English proof, especially for programmes taught in the local language or for those that include a language preparation year. These schemes frequently accept alternatives to IELTS.

Programmes with a language preparatory year

Where a scholarship includes a preparatory year in the local language, formal English testing may not be central to admission at all, since you will study in the host language.

Universities with flexible admissions policies

Individual universities set their own English policies, and many publish clear waiver conditions. Reading these policies carefully is the single most effective way to build a list of realistic scholarships without IELTS. How to build your no-IELTS application

Winning a funded place without IELTS is about presenting strong alternative evidence and a compelling overall application.

Step 1: Confirm the requirement in writing

Never rely on a forum post or a rumour. Read the official admissions page, and if the waiver conditions are unclear, email the admissions office and keep their reply. A written confirmation protects you from nasty surprises later.

Step 2: Gather your evidence early

Request your medium-of-instruction letter, gather transcripts that show English-taught courses, and collect any other proof of your English ability. Having these ready means you can apply the moment a suitable programme opens.

Step 3: Strengthen the rest of your application

When you skip IELTS, the rest of your application carries more weight. A clear, well-written statement of purpose demonstrates your English directly and shows your motivation. Step 4: Prepare for a possible interview

If a waiver depends on an interview, treat it seriously. Practise speaking about your background, your goals and your chosen field in English. Speaking confidently and clearly is exactly what the admissions team wants to see. Our AI advisor can help you rehearse common interview questions.

An IELTS waiver is a privilege, not a loophole. Universities grant it because they believe you can thrive in English. Your job is to prove that belief is justified.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Assuming a waiver applies. Policies vary by department and change over time. Always confirm.
  • Leaving evidence to the last minute. Official letters can take weeks to issue.
  • Neglecting your written English. If you skip a test, your essays must be flawless.
  • Ignoring alternative tests. Sometimes a cheaper, at-home test is the fastest path.
  • Applying to only one programme. Waiver-friendly programmes are plentiful, so apply widely.

How to request a medium-of-instruction letter

Because the medium-of-instruction letter is so valuable, it is worth knowing exactly how to obtain one. Contact the registrar, academic office or examinations office at the university where you studied. Explain that you need an official letter, on institutional letterhead and signed by an authorised officer, confirming that the language of instruction and assessment for your programme was English.

Ask for the letter to state the exact name of your degree, the dates you studied, and a clear statement that teaching and examinations were conducted in English. Request more than one original or certified copy if you plan to apply to several universities. Give the office plenty of time, because official documents can take days or weeks to produce, and a delay here can cost you a deadline elsewhere.

Regions and programmes known for flexibility

While policies are set institution by institution, some patterns are worth knowing when you build your shortlist.

Non-English-speaking European countries

Many universities in continental Europe run English-taught masters programmes and are pragmatic about proof of English, particularly where applicants studied their bachelor degree in English. Some accept a medium-of-instruction letter or an admissions interview in place of a formal test.

Programmes taught in a local language

Where a masters is delivered wholly in the host country's language, an English test may be irrelevant to admission. Instead, proof of proficiency in the language of instruction, or completion of a preparatory language year, becomes the relevant requirement.

Scholarships that assess English directly

Some funded programmes gauge your English through the application itself and an interview, judging your written statement and your spoken answers rather than a test score. For these, the quality of your application materials is your proof, so they must be excellent.

What if a programme still asks for some English proof?

Sometimes a waiver is partial: a university might drop the test requirement but still expect evidence of English somewhere in your file. In these cases, lean on the documents you already have. A medium-of-instruction letter, transcripts listing English-taught courses, prior work or study in English, and a flawless written statement together build a convincing picture. If a test is genuinely unavoidable, remember that IELTS is rarely the only accepted option, and a cheaper or at-home alternative may satisfy the requirement.

A practical application timeline

Approaching a no-IELTS application methodically keeps you ahead of deadlines:

  1. Months out: research programmes and note their exact English policies.
  2. Early on: request your medium-of-instruction letter and gather transcripts.
  3. Mid-process: draft and refine your statement of purpose until it is polished.
  4. Before submitting: confirm each programme's waiver conditions in writing.
  5. After applying: prepare for any interview that forms part of the assessment.

A structured tool makes it easier to compare waiver-friendly options side by side and keep track of which programmes fit your situation.

A realistic expectation

Studying abroad without IELTS is entirely possible, and many students do it every year. But approach it strategically. Target programmes whose published policies match your situation, prepare your alternative evidence early, and make the rest of your application excellent. Do that, and the lack of an IELTS score becomes a non-issue rather than an obstacle.

Understanding what English level universities expect

Even when a formal test is waived, universities still have a level of English in mind. Degrees involve reading dense material, writing essays or a dissertation, following fast-paced lectures and taking part in discussion. A waiver is the university's way of saying it believes your background already demonstrates that level, not that English does not matter.

This is why the alternatives all point back to genuine ability: a degree taught in English, a persuasive written statement, or a fluent interview. If you are honest with yourself that your English is strong enough for demanding academic work, a waiver route is a sound strategy. If you have doubts, investing time in your English before you apply will pay off both in admission and in your studies.

Free and low-cost ways to strengthen your English

Whether or not you take a test, improving your English strengthens every part of your application and prepares you for study abroad. You can build your skills without spending much:

  • Read widely in your field in English, from articles to textbooks, to absorb academic vocabulary and style.
  • Write regularly and ask a fluent friend or mentor to review your work, since your statement must be flawless.
  • Listen actively to lectures, talks and podcasts in your subject to train your ear for academic speech.
  • Practise speaking about your goals and your field aloud, so an interview feels natural rather than intimidating.

These habits cost little and compound over time, and they matter far more than a single number on a test report.

Putting it all together

The path to a funded place without IELTS is not a shortcut around English but a different way of proving it. Identify programmes whose published policies fit your background, secure your alternative evidence early, write a statement strong enough to speak for your English, and prepare for any interview. Do this across several programmes rather than pinning your hopes on one, and you give yourself a genuine, realistic route to fully funded study abroad. Frequently asked questions

Can I really get a fully funded scholarship without IELTS?+

Yes. Many fully funded programmes accept alternatives to IELTS, such as a medium-of-instruction letter confirming your previous degree was taught in English, an admissions interview, or another accepted English test. No IELTS does not mean no English requirement, only a different way of proving your English.

What is a medium-of-instruction letter?+

It is an official letter from your previous university, on letterhead, confirming that English was the language of teaching and assessment for your degree. It is one of the most powerful ways to waive IELTS, it is usually free to request, and you should get it early if your studies were in English.

Which scholarships are most likely to waive IELTS?+

Government scholarships in non-English-speaking countries, programmes that include a language preparatory year, and universities with published flexible admissions policies are the most likely to accept alternatives. Programmes taught in a local language often place little weight on IELTS at all.

Should I still improve my English if I skip IELTS?+

Absolutely. When you skip the test, the rest of your application, especially your written statement and any interview, carries more weight as proof of your English. Strong, clear English throughout your application is essential to convince admissions teams you can succeed.

How do I confirm that a programme will waive IELTS for me?+

Read the official admissions page and, if anything is unclear, email the admissions office directly and keep their written reply. Never rely on rumours or forum posts, because waiver policies differ by university and department and can change between cycles.
