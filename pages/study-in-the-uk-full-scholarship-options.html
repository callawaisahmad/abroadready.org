Studying in the UK on a Full Scholarship: Your Options

🗓️ Jun 15, 2026⏱️ 12 min read✍️ AbroadReady Team

Winning a study in the UK scholarship that covers your full costs is one of the most competitive but rewarding goals in international education. A genuine full ride to a British university can pay your tuition, give you a monthly living stipend, cover return flights, and sometimes even support your family. This guide walks through the major fully funded routes, who runs them, who qualifies, and how to build an application that stands out.

The UK hosts several of the world's oldest universities and some of the best-funded scholarship schemes anywhere. Whether you are aiming for a one-year master's or a fully funded PhD, there is likely a study in the UK scholarship designed with you in mind.

Key takeaways

  • The biggest fully funded routes are government-run: Chevening (master's) and the Commonwealth Scholarships (master's and PhD).
  • Cambridge and Oxford run their own flagship full awards: Gates Cambridge and the Rhodes and Clarendon scholarships.
  • Most full scholarships prioritise leadership, academic excellence, and a clear plan to give back to your home country.
  • Deadlines fall roughly 9 to 12 months before your course starts, so plan early.
  • You usually need a university place or offer, strong references, and a persuasive personal statement.

What fully funded really means in the UK

Not every award labelled a scholarship is a full ride. In UK terms, a full scholarship usually bundles together several components. Tuition fees are paid directly to the university. A monthly stipend covers rent, food, and daily living. Many schemes add a return economy airfare, an arrival allowance, and a thesis or study grant.

Partial awards, by contrast, might only discount tuition by a set amount or waive the international fee premium. Both are valuable, but if you cannot self-fund living costs, focus your energy on the genuinely full schemes below. Always read the funding breakdown carefully before you apply.

Chevening Scholarships

Chevening is the UK government's flagship international award, funded by the Foreign, Commonwealth and Development Office and partner organisations. It supports one-year master's degrees at any UK university. This is often the first study in the UK scholarship people think of, and for good reason.

What it covers

Chevening pays tuition, a monthly living allowance, return airfare, and additional grants for arrival and departure costs. It is a true full package for a taught master's.

Who should apply

Chevening targets emerging leaders with proven work experience, typically at least two years, and a clear vision for how their studies will benefit their home country. You apply to up to three master's courses, and you must hold an unconditional offer from one of them by a set deadline. Selection weighs leadership, networking ability, and a well-defined career plan as heavily as grades.

Commonwealth Scholarships

The Commonwealth Scholarship and Fellowship Plan, administered in the UK by the Commonwealth Scholarship Commission, funds students from eligible Commonwealth countries. Unlike Chevening, it covers both master's and PhD study, and it places special emphasis on candidates who might not otherwise afford to study abroad.

Awards cover tuition, a living stipend, airfare, and various grants. There are several streams, including master's scholarships, PhD scholarships, split-site PhD awards, and distance-learning scholarships for those who want a UK qualification without leaving home. Applicants from lower-income Commonwealth nations are prioritised, and development impact is central to selection.

Gates Cambridge Scholarship

Funded by a major donation from the Bill and Melinda Gates Foundation, the Gates Cambridge Scholarship supports outstanding postgraduate students from outside the UK to study at the University of Cambridge. It is among the most generous and prestigious awards available.

The scholarship covers the full cost of studying at Cambridge, including tuition, a maintenance allowance, and airfare, with additional discretionary funding for things like family support, fieldwork, and academic development. Selection looks for intellectual ability, a strong reason for choosing your course, a commitment to improving the lives of others, and leadership potential. You apply for your Cambridge course and the scholarship largely through the same postgraduate application system.

Rhodes Scholarship

The Rhodes Scholarship brings exceptional students to the University of Oxford. It is one of the oldest international scholarships in the world and is open to applicants from a defined and growing list of countries and regions.

Rhodes covers Oxford tuition and college fees plus a stipend for living costs, and it typically funds two years of study, with the possibility of a third in some cases. The Rhodes selection process is famously holistic. Committees look for academic excellence, energy to use your talents fully, truth and courage of character, and moral force combined with an instinct to lead. Applications are made through country-specific committees, each with its own deadline and process.

Oxford Clarendon and other university awards

Beyond the household names, individual universities run their own full or near-full scholarships. Oxford's Clarendon Fund offers around a thousand scholarships across graduate courses, covering fees and a generous grant for living costs. Most Clarendon awards are made automatically when you apply for your course by the funding deadline, so you do not submit a separate application.

Many other universities offer full or substantial awards for international students, especially at PhD level where funded studentships attached to research projects are common. Edinburgh, UCL, Warwick, Manchester, and others publish scholarship databases each year. When you shortlist universities, always read their dedicated funding pages, because a course-specific studentship can be easier to win than a national scheme. GREAT Scholarships and partial funding

The GREAT Scholarships programme, run by the British Council with participating universities, offers awards of a set amount toward tuition for students from selected countries. These are partial rather than full, but they can be combined with savings or other funding to make a UK degree affordable. If a full award does not come through, a stacked set of partial scholarships is a realistic Plan B.

How to build a competitive application

Full UK scholarships are heavily oversubscribed, so a strong application is not optional. A few principles separate winners from the rest.

Start with a clear why

Selection panels reward candidates who know exactly why they want a particular course at a particular university, and how it connects to a bigger goal. Vague ambition loses to specific, credible plans. Name the modules, the research group, or the professor whose work matches yours.

Show leadership and impact

Almost every major UK scheme, from Chevening to Rhodes, values leadership and service. Gather concrete examples where you led a team, ran a project, or created change, and be ready to quantify the outcome. Impact on your home country or community is a recurring theme.

Invest in your personal statement and references

Your statement is where you turn a list of achievements into a story. Draft it early, get feedback, and revise many times. Our statement of purpose builder can help you structure a compelling narrative and tighten your argument. Line up referees who know your work well and give them plenty of notice.

Meet every requirement precisely

Missing a document or a deadline is the most common reason strong candidates are rejected before they are ever read. Build a checklist for each scholarship, note the exact deadline in your time zone, and confirm your English language test meets the university requirement.

A realistic timeline

Most fully funded UK routes open applications up to a year before study begins. A workable sequence looks like this. Twelve to eighteen months out, research schemes and shortlist courses. Nine to twelve months out, sit any required tests and request references. Around the same time, submit university applications so you can secure an offer, since many scholarships require one. Then submit the scholarship application by its deadline, prepare for interviews, and wait for results, which often arrive a few months before your course starts.

Other routes and country-specific awards

The famous schemes are far from the only options. Several more routes are worth adding to your list, and casting a wide net is one of the smartest things you can do.

The Marshall Scholarship funds US citizens to study almost any subject at a UK university, covering tuition, a living stipend, and travel. The US-UK Fulbright Commission also supports American students and scholars. Beyond these, many national governments and private foundations co-fund UK study for their own citizens, so it is always worth checking whether your home country runs an overseas scholarship scheme that can be used at a British university. Subject-specific charities, professional bodies, and trusts add another layer, especially in fields like law, medicine, the arts, and engineering.

Funded PhD studentships and UKRI

At doctoral level, some of the most reliable money comes from the UK's Research Councils under UK Research and Innovation. These fund studentships through Doctoral Training Partnerships and Centres for Doctoral Training, usually attached to a specific project or cohort programme. A studentship typically covers fees plus a tax-free stipend for three to four years.

International students are eligible for a capped share of these places, so the competition is real but the awards are genuine full funding. The best way to find them is to search individual department and research group pages at your target universities, and to contact potential supervisors directly. A funded PhD in the UK is often won not through a national brand-name scheme but through matching your research to a project that already has money behind it.

What a stipend actually needs to cover

Cost of living varies enormously across the UK. London is far more expensive than most other cities, so a stipend that feels comfortable in Sheffield, Cardiff, or Belfast can be tight in the capital. When you compare offers, look at the stipend against the local cost of rent, utilities, food, and transport rather than the headline number alone.

Remember to budget for one-off and recurring costs that scholarships do not always cover in full, including visa fees, the Immigration Health Surcharge, and initial setup expenses when you arrive. Knowing what your award does and does not include prevents unpleasant surprises later.

Visas and the student route

Most scholarship holders study on the UK Student visa. You will generally need a Confirmation of Acceptance for Studies from your university, evidence of funds or your scholarship, and to meet health and other requirements. A full scholarship often satisfies the financial requirement automatically, which simplifies the process. If you plan to bring a partner or children, check carefully whether your specific award and course allow dependants, as rules have tightened for some categories.

Interviews: what to expect

Shortlisted candidates for schemes like Chevening, Rhodes, and Gates Cambridge usually face an interview, often before a panel. Interviewers probe your motivation, the coherence of your plan, your leadership experiences, and how you think under pressure. They may also ask about current issues in your field or your country.

The best preparation is to know your own application inside out, rehearse concise stories that show impact, and be ready to explain not just what you have done but what you intend to do next and why. Authenticity matters; panels can tell when answers are over-rehearsed or vague.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Applying to only one scheme instead of building a portfolio of applications.
  • Reusing a single generic essay across very different scholarships.
  • Leaving reference requests until the last minute, then getting rushed, thin letters.
  • Overlooking eligibility fine print such as nationality, minimum work experience, or a requirement to return home after study.
  • Underestimating how long it takes to book, sit, and receive results from an English language test.

If you are not selected: reapplying and alternatives

Rejection is common even for excellent candidates, simply because places are so limited. It is not the end of the road. Many successful scholars applied more than once, using feedback and experience to sharpen a second attempt. If a scheme allows reapplication, treat a near miss as useful data rather than a verdict on your ability.

In parallel, keep partial funding and self-funded options open. A combination of a GREAT Scholarship, a university bursary, family support, and part-time work can make a UK degree possible without a single full award. Flexibility about the university, city, or start year can also widen your options considerably.

Making the most of a shortlist

When you research schemes, keep a simple tracker with each scholarship, its eligibility, its deadline, the documents required, and whether it needs a university offer first. This turns a stressful scramble into a manageable project and ensures you never miss a requirement. Prioritise the schemes where you fit the criteria best, and give those your strongest effort while still applying broadly.

Final thoughts

A full study in the UK scholarship is achievable with early planning, a sharp application, and honest self-assessment about where you are competitive. Aim high with Chevening, Commonwealth, Gates Cambridge, or Rhodes, but also apply for university-specific awards where the odds can be friendlier. Apply broadly, tailor every application, and give yourself the runway to do it well.

Frequently asked questions

Can international students get a full scholarship to study in the UK?+

Yes. Fully funded routes such as Chevening, the Commonwealth Scholarships, Gates Cambridge, and the Rhodes Scholarship cover tuition, living costs, and flights. They are highly competitive, so applying to several and preparing early improves your chances.

Do I need a university offer before I apply for a scholarship?+

It depends on the scheme. Chevening requires an unconditional offer from a UK master's course by a set deadline, while Gates Cambridge and Clarendon are largely tied to your course application itself. Always check each scheme's exact rules.

Which is the best full scholarship for a master's in the UK?+

For a one-year taught master's, Chevening is the most prominent government award, while Commonwealth Scholarships suit applicants from eligible Commonwealth countries. The best fit depends on your nationality, work experience, and career goals.

When should I start applying for UK scholarships?+

Begin 12 to 18 months before your intended start date. Most fully funded schemes have deadlines 9 to 12 months ahead, and you often need time to secure a university offer, sit English tests, and gather references.

Are there full scholarships for a PhD in the UK?+

Yes. Commonwealth PhD Scholarships, Gates Cambridge, the Rhodes Scholarship, Oxford's Clarendon Fund, and many university-based funded studentships all support doctoral study with tuition and a stipend.
