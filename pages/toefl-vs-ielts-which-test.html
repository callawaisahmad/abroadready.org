TOEFL vs IELTS: Which English Test Should You Take?

🗓️ Mar 27, 2026⏱️ 11 min read✍️ AbroadReady Team

The TOEFL vs IELTS question is one of the first decisions international students face, and choosing well can save you time, money, and stress. Both tests measure academic English and both are accepted by thousands of universities worldwide, but they differ in format, style, and the type of candidate they suit. This guide breaks down the TOEFL vs IELTS comparison across everything that actually matters so you can pick the test that plays to your strengths rather than fighting against them.

There is no universally easier test. The right answer depends on your English background, how you handle a computer versus a face-to-face examiner, and what your target institutions accept. Let us work through it methodically.

Key takeaways

  • Both TOEFL and IELTS are widely accepted; always confirm what your specific programme requires first.
  • The core TOEFL vs IELTS difference is style: TOEFL is fully computer-based and American-leaning, while IELTS Academic offers a human Speaking examiner.
  • TOEFL Speaking is recorded into a microphone; IELTS Speaking is a live conversation, which suits different personalities.
  • IELTS uses a 0 to 9 band scale; TOEFL iBT is scored out of 120.
  • Choose based on your strengths, comfort with technology, and destination, not on rumours about difficulty.

The basics of each test

The TOEFL iBT is produced by ETS and is delivered almost entirely online, either at a test centre or at home. It leans towards North American English and academic contexts, with lecture-style listening passages and integrated tasks that combine skills.

The IELTS is jointly run by the British Council, IDP, and Cambridge. The Academic version, which most applicants take, uses a mix of British and international English. Its defining feature is a face-to-face Speaking test with a real examiner, which no other major test offers in the same way.

Who accepts which test

Acceptance is rarely the deciding factor anymore because the vast majority of universities in the US, UK, Canada, Australia, and Europe accept both. That said, some specific programmes, professional bodies, or immigration routes prefer one. The golden rule in any TOEFL vs IELTS decision is to check the exact requirement for your target course before you book anything.

Format and structure compared

Both tests assess Reading, Listening, Writing, and Speaking, but the experience of each differs meaningfully.

Reading

TOEFL Reading uses long academic passages with multiple-choice questions, all on screen. IELTS Academic Reading offers a wider variety of question types, including matching headings, True/False/Not Given, and sentence completion, which some candidates find more varied and others find fiddly.

Listening

TOEFL Listening features university lectures and campus conversations, and you take notes then answer afterward. IELTS Listening includes everyday and academic scenarios, and you answer as you listen, which rewards candidates who process information in real time.

Writing

TOEFL Writing includes an integrated task, where you read, listen, then write a response combining both sources, plus an independent essay. IELTS Academic Writing has Task 1, describing a chart, graph, or process, and Task 2, an opinion or argument essay. If you dislike interpreting data visuals, that IELTS Task 1 is worth weighing carefully.

Speaking

This is the sharpest difference in the whole TOEFL vs IELTS comparison. TOEFL Speaking is delivered by speaking into a microphone, with your responses recorded and scored later, sometimes partly by automated systems. IELTS Speaking is a live, three-part conversation with a human examiner. Introverts and those who freeze when recorded often prefer the human interaction of IELTS, while people who dislike being watched sometimes prefer the privacy of the TOEFL microphone.

Scoring: understanding the two scales

IELTS reports each section and an overall score on a 0 to 9 band scale, in half-band increments. Most competitive universities ask for an overall 6.5 to 7.5, often with minimums in each section.

TOEFL iBT scores each section from 0 to 30, giving a total out of 120. Typical requirements sit around 80 to 100 for many programmes, with more selective courses asking for higher. Because the scales are so different, it helps to convert your target university requirement into both systems before deciding, so you know which score feels more attainable for you.

Which test is easier?

There is no honest single answer, but there are patterns. TOEFL tends to suit candidates who are comfortable with academic, lecture-heavy content, typing quickly, and working entirely on a computer for several hours. Its question types are more predictable and repetitive, which some learners find easier to drill.

IELTS tends to suit candidates who prefer variety, who write comfortably by hand or on screen, and who perform better talking to a real person. Its wider range of question types can feel less monotonous, though it means more formats to master.

Ask yourself one question: do I perform better speaking to a human being or to a microphone? Your honest answer settles the Speaking section, which is where many candidates gain or lose the most.

Practical factors: cost, availability, and results

Pricing for both tests is broadly comparable and varies by country, so check current local fees rather than relying on old figures. Availability also depends on where you live; in some regions IELTS has more test dates and centres, in others TOEFL is easier to book, including its at-home option.

Results turnaround is fast for both, typically within a couple of weeks, and both offer computer-delivered IELTS options in many locations that speed things up. If you are on a tight application deadline, availability and result speed in your specific city may matter more than any format preference.

How to make your decision

Work through these steps in order. First, confirm which tests your target universities and any funders accept, and note the exact score required. Second, take a free sample or diagnostic of each test to feel the difference directly rather than relying on rumours. Third, weigh your personal strengths: comfort with technology, handwriting versus typing, and human versus recorded Speaking. Fourth, check practical logistics of cost and test dates in your area.

A quick decision summary

Lean towards TOEFL if you are comfortable with computers, American-style academic content, typed essays, and speaking into a microphone, or if your destination is primarily North America. Lean towards IELTS if you prefer variety in question types, a live human Speaking examiner, and if you are heading to the UK, Australia, or many European programmes. In the end, the best result in the TOEFL vs IELTS debate comes from choosing the test that fits how you naturally perform, then preparing thoroughly for it.

Frequently asked questions

Do universities prefer TOEFL or IELTS?+

Most universities accept both equally, so preference rarely decides anything. A minority of specific programmes, professional registrations, or immigration routes favour one, so always check the exact requirement for your target course before booking.

Is TOEFL or IELTS easier to get a high score in?+

Neither is universally easier. TOEFL suits candidates comfortable with computers, typing, and American academic content, while IELTS suits those who prefer varied question types and a live Speaking examiner. The easier test is the one that matches your strengths.

How are the TOEFL and IELTS scores different?+

IELTS is scored on a 0 to 9 band scale in half-band steps, while TOEFL iBT is scored out of 120, with 30 points per section. Convert your target university requirement into both scales before deciding which target feels more achievable.

Can I switch between TOEFL and IELTS if I do poorly?+

Yes. Scores from both are independent, so you can retake either test or switch entirely. However, switching means learning a new format, so it is usually smarter to identify the better-fitting test from the start.

Which test has a better Speaking format?+

That depends on you. IELTS uses a live conversation with a human examiner, which many people find more natural. TOEFL records your spoken answers into a microphone, which suits candidates who prefer privacy over face-to-face interaction.
