Scholarships to Study in Canada: A Complete Guide

🗓️ Jun 10, 2026⏱️ 12 min read✍️ AbroadReady Team

If you are searching for study in Canada scholarships, it helps to understand how funding works there. Canada is a top destination for international students thanks to its research strength, welcoming immigration pathways, and high quality of life. Funding, however, is organised differently from many countries: rather than one giant national scholarship, support is spread across federal programmes, provincial awards, and generous university scholarships. This guide maps the whole landscape so you know where to look.

Whether you want an undergraduate award, a funded master's, or a fully financed PhD, there are real study in Canada scholarships for international students. The key is knowing which door to knock on.

Key takeaways

  • Canada's biggest funding sits at graduate level, especially for research master's and PhD study.
  • The Vanier Canada Graduate Scholarships are the flagship federal award for doctoral students.
  • Many of the best undergraduate awards, like Toronto's Lester B. Pearson scholarship, come from universities directly.
  • PhD students are often funded through a mix of supervisor grants, teaching assistantships, and internal awards.
  • Apply to the university first, then chase the scholarships attached to it, since most awards require admission.

How scholarship funding works in Canada

Unlike countries with a single national scheme, Canadian funding comes from three main layers. Federal programmes are run by national research agencies and target graduate researchers. Provincial programmes, such as those in Ontario, add another tier of support. Finally, individual universities offer the widest range of awards, from entrance scholarships to fully funded doctoral packages.

For international students, this means the university you choose matters enormously. A strong department may bundle admission with a funding offer, especially for research degrees. Always read a program's funding page alongside its admission requirements.

Vanier Canada Graduate Scholarships

The Vanier Canada Graduate Scholarships are the country's premier doctoral award, designed to attract world-class PhD students to Canadian universities. They are open to both domestic and international candidates.

Vanier awards provide a substantial annual stipend for three years of doctoral study. Selection rests on three pillars: academic excellence, research potential, and leadership. Importantly, you do not apply directly. You must first be nominated by a Canadian university, so the process starts with contacting a department and a potential supervisor well ahead of the internal deadline. If a fully funded PhD is your goal, Vanier should be near the top of your list of study in Canada scholarships.

Banting Postdoctoral Fellowships

For those looking beyond the PhD, the Banting Postdoctoral Fellowships support top-tier postdoctoral researchers, again with a competitive stipend and a nomination-based process through a host institution. While not for degree students, they are worth noting if you plan a long research career in Canada.

University entrance and merit scholarships

Universities are where most international students actually find funding, particularly at the undergraduate level. Several flagship programmes stand out.

Lester B. Pearson International Scholarship

The University of Toronto's Lester B. Pearson International Scholarship is one of the most generous undergraduate awards in the country. It covers tuition, books, incidental fees, and full residence support for four years, making it effectively a full ride. It recognises exceptional academic achievement and creativity, alongside strong recognition as a leader within your school. Candidates are usually nominated by their school before applying.

Other university awards

Many institutions run their own entrance awards for international students. UBC offers International Scholars awards for outstanding students who demonstrate financial need. Waterloo, McGill, Alberta, and others publish international entrance scholarships each cycle. Some are automatic based on your admission grades, while others need a separate application or essay. Because criteria and amounts vary widely, check each university's international scholarship page carefully. You can browse and filter current opportunities on our scholarship results page.

Provincial and graduate awards

Provinces add another layer of graduate funding. The Ontario Graduate Scholarship, for example, supports master's and doctoral students at Ontario universities and is open to international students in many cases, funded jointly by the province and the institution. Quebec and other provinces run comparable programmes. These awards are typically administered by your university's graduate school, so the application flows through them.

How PhD and research master's funding really works

Here is a crucial point many applicants miss. At the PhD level, and often for research-based master's degrees, funding in Canada is frequently built into the admission offer rather than won through a standalone scholarship. A typical funded package might combine a research assistantship paid from your supervisor's grant, a teaching assistantship, and one or more internal awards or fellowships.

This is why finding and contacting a potential supervisor early is so important for research students. A professor who wants to work with you and has grant money can be the difference between a funded place and an unfunded one. When you email a prospective supervisor, be specific about your research interests and how they align with their lab. A well-crafted statement of purpose helps here too, and our statement of purpose builder can help you shape one that lands.

Finding the right fit

Because Canadian funding is decentralised, matching your profile to the right programme is half the battle. Consider your level of study, your field, and whether you are pursuing a taught or research degree. Undergraduates should target university entrance awards. Master's and PhD applicants should look at federal and provincial schemes plus departmental funding, and prioritise programmes with a track record of supporting international students. How to strengthen your application

Whatever the scholarship, a few habits improve your odds significantly.

Apply early and to the university first

Most Canadian scholarships require you to be admitted or nominated, so the university application is your gateway. Submit it well before deadlines, especially since some scholarship nominations happen automatically at the point of admission.

Build a strong academic and leadership record

Federal awards like Vanier weigh leadership heavily, and top undergraduate awards look for students who lead and contribute beyond the classroom. Document your achievements, roles, and impact clearly.

Secure excellent references

Strong, specific reference letters carry real weight, particularly for research awards where a supervisor's endorsement signals your potential. Give referees time and context so they can write in detail.

Tailor every application

Generic essays rarely win competitive awards. Connect your goals to the specific programme, department, and country, and show why Canada in particular fits your plans.

The Trudeau Foundation and other doctoral awards

Beyond Vanier, Canada offers a small number of prestigious doctoral awards. The Pierre Elliott Trudeau Foundation Scholarships support outstanding PhD students in the social sciences and humanities whose work engages with pressing social issues, adding funding plus a leadership and mentorship programme. These awards are highly selective and tend to suit engaged, publicly minded researchers.

Some universities also run their own flagship doctoral fellowships that rival national schemes in value. When you research programs, look for named graduate fellowships, presidential scholarships, or international doctoral awards, which can stack with departmental funding to build a strong overall package.

Understanding federal research agency funding

Canada's three federal research agencies fund a lot of graduate research, but it is important to understand how this reaches international students. Much of the core graduate scholarship funding at master's level is directed toward domestic students, while the Vanier program is explicitly open to international candidates at the doctoral level.

In practice, this means international students usually access agency money indirectly, through supervisors who hold research grants and pay assistantships from them. That is another reason the supervisor relationship is so central: a well-funded lab can support you even where a direct scholarship is not open to you.

Affordability, work rights, and the bigger picture

Scholarships are only part of the affordability equation. Canada allows eligible international students to work a limited number of hours during their studies, which can supplement a partial scholarship or savings. Costs vary widely by city, with the largest metros being noticeably more expensive than smaller university towns, so factor local rent and living costs into your plans.

Canada is also known for post-graduation work options that can lead to longer-term pathways, which is part of why it is such a popular destination. When you weigh an offer, consider not just the scholarship value but the total cost of living and the opportunities that follow graduation.

Comparing offers and stacking funding

Because funding is decentralised, it is common to end up comparing quite different offers: one university might grant a large entrance scholarship, another a smaller award plus a guaranteed assistantship. Read each offer carefully to see what is guaranteed versus conditional, whether funding is renewable each year, and what academic standards you must maintain to keep it.

Many students combine sources, pairing a university award with an assistantship, an external scholarship from their home country, and part-time work. A stacked approach can turn a partial award into a fully funded reality.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Assuming there is one big national scholarship rather than researching university and provincial options.
  • Applying to programs without contacting potential supervisors, which is critical for research funding.
  • Missing early admission deadlines that also gate automatic scholarship consideration.
  • Overlooking renewal conditions and the grades needed to keep an award.
  • Ignoring the difference between a guaranteed funding package and a one-off entrance scholarship.

Writing a supervisor email that gets a reply

For research applicants, the email to a potential supervisor is one of the most important messages you will send. Professors receive many generic requests, so specificity is what earns a reply. Keep it concise and professional, and show that you have read their recent work.

State clearly who you are, the degree you are seeking, and why their research aligns with yours. Reference a specific project or paper, propose a rough direction for your own work, and attach a short CV and a brief research summary. Ask a focused question, such as whether they are taking new students and whether funding might be available. A tailored, well-researched message stands out immediately from copy-and-paste requests.

External and home-country funding

Not all funding for Canadian study originates in Canada. Many governments, foundations, and employers sponsor citizens to study abroad, and these awards can often be used at Canadian universities. Check whether your home country runs an overseas scholarship scheme, and look into professional bodies or charities connected to your field.

These external awards can be combined with a Canadian university scholarship or an assistantship, and because they draw on a different applicant pool, they can be a strong complement to your main applications. Casting a wide net across both Canadian and home-country sources meaningfully improves your odds of full funding.

Deadlines and the application calendar

Because Canadian scholarships are tied to admission and nomination, your calendar should start with university application deadlines, then work backward. Standardised tests, English proficiency exams, transcripts, and reference letters all take time to organise. Build in buffer weeks so that a delayed transcript or a slow referee does not derail an otherwise strong application. Meeting the earliest relevant deadline often maximises your scholarship consideration, since some awards are decided automatically at admission.

Choosing the right province and university

Where you study in Canada affects both your funding options and your costs. Different provinces run different graduate scholarship schemes, and tuition and living expenses vary widely between regions and between large cities and smaller towns. A university in a mid-sized city may offer a more affordable overall package even if its headline scholarship is smaller.

Look for institutions with a strong record of supporting international students in your field, generous entrance or graduate awards, and, for research degrees, active supervisors with funding. Balancing scholarship value, cost of living, program quality, and post-study opportunities gives you a clearer picture than focusing on any single factor. Investing time in this comparison early often pays off more than rushing many applications to poorly matched programs.

Final thoughts

The search for study in Canada scholarships rewards students who understand the system. Because funding is spread across federal, provincial, and university sources, your best strategy is to choose universities with strong support for international students, apply early, and pursue several awards at once. For research degrees, an engaged supervisor is often your single most valuable asset. Plan ahead, apply widely, and Canada's excellent institutions become genuinely affordable.

Frequently asked questions

Are there full scholarships to study in Canada for international students?+

Yes, though they are competitive. The University of Toronto's Lester B. Pearson scholarship is effectively a full undergraduate ride, and Vanier Canada Graduate Scholarships fully fund PhD study. Many research students are also fully funded through admission packages.

How do I get funding for a PhD in Canada?+

Most PhD funding is built into your admission offer, combining research and teaching assistantships with internal awards. Contacting a potential supervisor early is essential, and top students can also be nominated for the Vanier Canada Graduate Scholarships.

Do I apply to the scholarship or the university first?+

Usually the university first. Most Canadian scholarships require you to be admitted or nominated, and some awards are granted automatically at admission based on your grades, so meeting university deadlines is critical.

Can undergraduates get scholarships in Canada?+

Yes. Many universities offer entrance and merit scholarships for international undergraduates, some automatic and some requiring a separate essay or nomination. The Lester B. Pearson International Scholarship is among the most generous.

What is the Vanier Canada Graduate Scholarship?+

It is Canada's flagship doctoral award, offering a substantial annual stipend for three years. It is judged on academic excellence, research potential, and leadership, and you must be nominated by a Canadian university rather than applying directly.
