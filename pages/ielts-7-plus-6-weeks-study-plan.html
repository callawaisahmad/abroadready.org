IELTS 7+ in 6 Weeks: A Realistic IELTS Study Plan

🗓️ Apr 1, 2026⏱️ 12 min read✍️ AbroadReady Team

Reaching Band 7 on the IELTS is a common requirement for competitive universities and scholarships, and it is achievable in six weeks if you already sit somewhere around Band 6 to 6.5. This IELTS study plan gives you a realistic, week-by-week structure rather than vague advice to simply practise more. It assumes you can commit roughly 1.5 to 2 hours on weekdays and a longer block at weekends. If your current level is closer to Band 5, treat this as a strong foundation and expect to extend the timeline.

The honest truth first: a good IELTS study plan is not about learning English from scratch. It is about learning the test, closing specific skill gaps, and building the stamina to perform for nearly three hours. Band 7 rewards accuracy, range, and control, not just fluency.

Key takeaways

  • Band 7 in six weeks is realistic if you start around Band 6 to 6.5 and study consistently.
  • Take a full timed diagnostic test before you plan anything, so your IELTS study plan targets real weaknesses.
  • Writing and Speaking are where most people lose marks; they need active feedback, not passive practice.
  • Reading and Listening improve fastest through timed practice plus error analysis.
  • Simulate full test conditions in the final two weeks to build stamina and reduce exam-day surprises.

Understand what Band 7 actually requires

Each of the four sections is scored from 0 to 9, and your overall band is the average, rounded to the nearest half. To land a 7 overall you generally need 6.5 to 7.5 across the board, so a single weak section can drag you down. Many universities also set a minimum in each section, so aim for balance rather than one heroic score.

Band 7 descriptors reward specific things. In Writing, it means clear organisation, a range of sentence structures, and only occasional errors. In Speaking, it means fluency without long pauses, flexible vocabulary, and generally accurate grammar. Knowing the descriptors turns your IELTS study plan from guesswork into targeted work.

Choose Academic or General Training early

Most university and scholarship applicants take the Academic version. The Listening and Speaking sections are identical, but Reading and Writing differ. If you are applying to study or for a scholarship, confirm you are booked for Academic before you spend six weeks practising the wrong Task 1.

Week 0: The diagnostic weekend

Before week one begins, sit one full official practice test under timed conditions. Do not pause, do not look up words, and do not give yourself extra minutes. This is the single most valuable hour of your whole preparation because it tells you where you really stand.

Mark it honestly. For Reading and Listening, count your raw score and convert it to a band. For Writing and Speaking, compare your work against the public band descriptors, or better, get feedback from a teacher or a reliable tool. Record four numbers, one per section. That baseline shapes everything that follows.

Weeks 1 to 2: Build accuracy and fix the basics

The first fortnight is about diagnosis becoming action. Spend your sessions roughly like this: Reading and Listening on alternating days, Writing twice a week, and Speaking in short daily bursts.

Listening

Do one section per day and always review your mistakes. The most common losses come from spelling, plurals, and losing your place when the speaker paraphrases. Practise predicting answers before the audio plays, and train yourself to keep listening after you miss one, rather than freezing.

Reading

In these two weeks, prioritise technique over speed. Learn to scan for keywords, recognise paraphrase, and handle True/False/Not Given, which trips up even strong readers. Time each passage at 20 minutes so the clock becomes normal early.

Writing

Write one Task 1 and one Task 2 this fortnight, but spend more time analysing model answers than producing volume. Learn the structure of a Band 7 essay: a clear position, two developed body paragraphs, and a conclusion. For Academic Task 1, build a bank of phrases for describing trends, comparisons, and processes.

Speaking

Record yourself answering Part 1 questions daily. Listen back for hesitation and repeated words. Two minutes a day of self-recording exposes habits you cannot hear in the moment.

Weeks 3 to 4: Expand range and add pressure

By now the format should feel familiar. The middle two weeks push range: more sophisticated vocabulary, more varied grammar, and faster processing. Increase Reading and Listening to full sections under time pressure, and step up Writing feedback.

Grow vocabulary in context

Do not memorise word lists in isolation. Instead, collect topic vocabulary around common IELTS themes such as education, environment, technology, health, and urban life. For each theme, note five collocations you can actually deploy in an essay or a Speaking answer. Range with control beats rare words used incorrectly.

Target Writing Task 2 relentlessly

Task 2 carries more weight than Task 1, so give it more attention now. Write two essays a week, each fully timed at 40 minutes. After each one, check three things: did you answer the exact question, are your paragraphs unified around one idea, and did you vary your sentence structures. Grammatical range and accuracy is a full 25 percent of your Writing score, so mix simple, compound, and complex sentences deliberately.

The fastest way to lose a Writing band is to write a memorised essay that does not address the specific question. Examiners are trained to spot this instantly.

Speaking fluency drills

Move to full Part 2 long-turn practice. Use the one-minute preparation to jot keywords, then speak for the full two minutes without stopping. If you run dry, add examples, contrasts, or a short personal story. Fluency is partly a stamina skill, and two minutes feels long until you practise it a dozen times.

Weeks 5 to 6: Simulate, refine, and peak

The final fortnight is about performance, not new learning. Sit two full timed mock tests, ideally on the weekends, in one sitting with realistic breaks. This builds the concentration stamina that separates a real Band 7 from someone who scores well on individual sections but fades during the real thing.

Between mocks, do focused repair work on your weakest section only. If Listening Part 4 keeps costing you, drill Part 4. If Writing coherence is the issue, rewrite two old essays with better linking. Resist the urge to keep practising what you are already good at because it feels comfortable.

Refine your exam-day routine

Decide your section-by-section timing in advance. In Reading, that usually means 20 minutes per passage and never leaving a blank answer. In Writing, spend around 20 minutes on Task 1 and 40 on Task 2, with five minutes at the end to check for slips. Rehearsing timing removes decisions from the day itself.

Section strategies that reliably add marks

Listening

Read the questions before each recording and underline keywords. Watch for distractors, where the speaker mentions a wrong answer before correcting it. Transfer answers carefully and never leave a blank, since there is no penalty for guessing.

Reading

Do not read every word. Skim for structure, then scan for the specific information each question needs. Manage your time so no single passage steals minutes from another, and remember that questions usually follow the order of the text.

Writing

Plan for two minutes before you write. A short plan prevents the mid-essay wandering that wrecks coherence. Use clear paragraphing, a range of linking devices used naturally, and precise vocabulary. Always leave time to proofread for articles, plurals, and verb tenses.

Speaking

Extend every answer with a reason and an example. Treat the examiner as a curious conversation partner, not an interrogator. It is fine to correct yourself, and a natural pause to think reads far better than filler noise.

How to know your plan is working

Track your mock band scores across the six weeks. You should see steady, uneven progress, with Reading and Listening often rising first because they respond quickly to practice. Writing and Speaking tend to move more slowly because they depend on feedback and habit change. If a section flatlines for two weeks, change your method rather than simply repeating it.

Once your target scores are locked in, that same discipline transfers directly to the rest of your application. Common mistakes that keep people at Band 6.5

Three habits stall applicants just short of Band 7. First, passive practice, where you do test after test without ever analysing errors. Second, memorising essays and answers, which examiners penalise. Third, ignoring one weak section until it quietly caps your overall score. A disciplined IELTS study plan attacks all three by pairing practice with reflection and by balancing effort across sections.

Stay consistent, review every mistake, and simulate the real thing before test day. Six focused weeks of that will move most Band 6.5 candidates comfortably into Band 7 territory.

Frequently asked questions

Can I really improve by a full band in six weeks?+

Moving from around 6 to 6.5 up to a 7 is realistic in six weeks with consistent, focused study and honest error analysis. Jumping two full bands, for example from 5 to 7, usually needs longer because it involves fundamental language growth, not just test technique.

How many hours a day should I study for IELTS?+

Around 1.5 to 2 hours on weekdays plus a longer weekend block works well for most working applicants. Quality matters more than quantity: one hour of reviewing your mistakes is worth more than three hours of untargeted practice.

Which section is hardest to improve quickly?+

Writing and Speaking usually improve the slowest because they depend on feedback and changing ingrained habits. Reading and Listening tend to rise faster because timed practice and error review produce quick, measurable gains.

Should I take the Academic or General Training test?+

Almost all university and scholarship applicants need the Academic version. Confirm the exact requirement with your institution before booking, because the Reading and Writing sections differ between the two versions.

Is it worth memorising sample answers?+

No. Examiners are trained to detect memorised responses, and using one that does not fit the specific question will lower your score. Learn flexible structures and vocabulary instead, so you can adapt to whatever question appears.
