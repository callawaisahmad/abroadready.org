Global Korea Scholarship (GKS): The Complete Guide

🗓️ May 26, 2026⏱️ 12 min read✍️ AbroadReady Team

The Global Korea Scholarship, often abbreviated as GKS and formerly known as the Korean Government Scholarship Program, is South Korea's flagship fully funded scholarship for international students. Administered by the National Institute for International Education, it supports both undergraduate and graduate study at universities across Korea, covering tuition, living costs, airfare, and a year of Korean language training. This guide breaks down how it works, who can apply, what it covers, and how to build a competitive application.

If you dream of studying in Korea without the financial burden, the Global Korea Scholarship is the single most important program to understand. It is generous, well organised, and open to students from many countries.

Key takeaways

  • GKS is a fully funded Korean government scholarship for undergraduate and graduate study.
  • It covers tuition, a monthly living stipend, airfare, medical insurance, and a year of Korean language training.
  • You apply through one of two tracks: the Embassy track or the University track.
  • You can choose only one track and, in most cases, apply to a limited number of universities.
  • A settlement allowance and a degree completion grant are also part of the package.

What the Global Korea Scholarship covers

GKS is one of the most complete scholarship packages available anywhere. While exact figures are set each year, the benefits generally include full tuition, a monthly living allowance, a settlement allowance on arrival, medical insurance, and a round-trip airfare. Recipients also receive one year of Korean language training at a designated institution before beginning their degree, plus a degree completion grant when they finish. Graduate students may receive a research allowance, and those who achieve a high level of Korean proficiency can qualify for an additional language proficiency grant.

Because the amounts are reviewed periodically, always confirm the current benefits in the official application guidelines for your year and study level.

Undergraduate and graduate tracks

GKS is split into two main degree levels, each with its own guidelines and timeline.

Undergraduate GKS

This funds a full bachelor's degree in Korea, preceded by one year of Korean language study. It is aimed at students who have completed or will complete secondary education and want to pursue their entire undergraduate degree in Korea.

Graduate GKS

This funds master's and doctoral degrees, and in some cases research programs, again with a year of Korean language study beforehand. It is designed for applicants who hold or are completing a relevant prior degree and want to continue their studies in Korea.

The two application tracks

Choosing between the Embassy track and the University track is the most important decision in your Global Korea Scholarship application. You may only apply through one track in a given cycle.

Embassy track

In the Embassy track, you apply through the Korean embassy in your home country. The embassy conducts the first round of selection, and successful candidates then apply to a limited number of universities. This track is often chosen by applicants who want flexibility in choosing universities and are confident in a broad, competitive selection process. Because you are competing within your country's quota, the field can be strong, so a polished application matters.

University track

In the University track, you apply directly to a participating Korean university, which conducts the selection and recommends you to the program. This track can suit applicants who have a specific university and program in mind, particularly graduate students who want to align with a certain department or supervisor. Universities set their own internal requirements and deadlines within the overall program framework.

How to choose your track

There is no universally better track. The Embassy track can offer a sense of national competition and, for some, clearer procedures, while the University track lets you target a specific institution and, for graduate applicants, build a relationship with a department. Consider your priorities: if a particular university or professor is central to your plans, the University track may fit better. If you value flexibility across universities, the Embassy track may suit you. Whichever you choose, read the official guidelines for that track carefully, as documents and deadlines differ. Eligibility essentials

While detailed criteria are published each year, a few common requirements apply. Applicants must generally hold the nationality of an eligible country and not hold Korean citizenship. There are age guidelines that differ by degree level, and a minimum academic performance threshold, often expressed as a grade point average percentile. Applicants must also be in good health. Meeting the baseline is only the start, since selection is competitive and considers your whole profile.

Building a strong GKS application

GKS attracts many applicants, so a thoughtful, well-prepared application is essential.

Write compelling personal and study statements

Your personal statement and statement of purpose are central to the application. They should convey your motivation for studying in Korea, your academic and career goals, and why your chosen program fits. Be specific and authentic rather than generic. Our statement of purpose builder can help you structure a clear, persuasive narrative.

Prepare a strong study or research plan

Graduate applicants in particular should present a clear study or research plan that shows focus and feasibility. Connect your plan to the strengths of your target program and, where relevant, a potential supervisor.

Secure detailed recommendation letters

Recommendation letters carry real weight. Choose referees who know your work well and can speak specifically to your abilities and potential, and give them enough time and context to write in detail.

Get your documents right

GKS requires a set of documents including application forms, transcripts, certificates, statements, recommendation letters, and sometimes proof of language ability. Some documents may need to be apostilled or notarised, which takes time, so start early and follow the checklist for your track precisely.

Consider Korean language ability

While GKS includes a year of Korean language training and you do not need fluency to apply, demonstrating some Korean or English proficiency can strengthen your application. A recognised Korean proficiency level can also unlock additional benefits once you are in the program.

A realistic timeline

GKS runs on an annual cycle. The Embassy track typically opens earlier in the year, while the University track timing varies by institution. In broad terms, expect application windows in the first half of the year, selection over the following months, and enrolment beginning with language training later in the year or the next spring. Because documents and translations take time, begin preparing months before the window opens. Understanding the quota system

GKS operates on a quota system that shapes your odds. In the Embassy track, each country is allocated a certain number of slots, so you are effectively competing against other applicants from your own country for those places. In the University track, participating universities receive their own allocations and select candidates directly.

This distinction matters when you choose a track. Some applicants from very competitive countries prefer the University track because they can target an institution where their profile stands out, while others value the structured, nationally administered Embassy track. There is no single right answer; the best choice depends on your background, your target program, and where you believe you are most competitive.

The Korean language requirement in practice

Every GKS scholar completes one year of Korean language training before starting their degree, and there is usually a proficiency benchmark you must reach, measured by the Test of Proficiency in Korean, known as TOPIK. Reaching the required level is a genuine milestone, and scholars who arrive with some Korean often find the year less pressured.

Some graduate programs taught in English may allow flexibility, and achieving a higher TOPIK level can unlock additional financial benefits. Whatever your starting point, treat the language year as a serious part of the program rather than a formality, because it underpins your success in the degree that follows.

Choosing your universities and programs

GKS involves selecting from participating universities, and there are often limits on how many you can list and which combinations are allowed. Universities can be grouped in ways that affect your choices, so read the guidelines for your track and year carefully before finalising your list.

Choose programs where your background genuinely fits, and, for graduate study, where a department's strengths match your research interests. A focused, well-justified choice reads far better than a scattergun list, and it also sets you up for a more rewarding experience once you arrive.

The health check and other requirements

GKS includes a health requirement, with applicants completing a personal medical assessment during the application and a fuller check later in the process. Serious health conditions that would prevent study can affect eligibility, so it is worth being aware of this step early.

You will also encounter document formalities such as notarisation or apostille for certain records, and specific formatting rules for forms and essays. These administrative details are easy to underestimate, and getting them wrong can cost you, so build in time to prepare everything correctly.

Life in Korea and what the stipend covers

The monthly living allowance is designed to cover everyday costs, and combined with the settlement allowance, medical insurance, and tuition coverage, it makes a Korean degree genuinely affordable. Living costs vary between Seoul and smaller cities, so your budget will stretch further outside the capital.

Many scholars find the overall package generous enough to focus fully on study and language learning. As always, understanding local costs before you arrive helps you plan, especially for your initial setup and accommodation.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Applying through both tracks, which is not allowed, instead of committing to one.
  • Treating the required documents casually and missing notarisation or formatting rules.
  • Writing generic statements that do not connect to Korea or your chosen program.
  • Underestimating the Korean language benchmark you must reach during the training year.
  • Choosing universities at random rather than matching programs to your goals.

Alternatives to GKS

GKS is the flagship, but it is not the only way to fund study in Korea. Many Korean universities offer their own scholarships for international students, ranging from partial to full tuition waivers, and some are awarded automatically based on your grades. These can be an excellent backup or even a first choice if you have a specific university in mind.

Local governments, foundations, and specific departments also provide support, and English-taught graduate programs sometimes come with attractive funding to attract international talent. If you are not selected for the government scheme, a well-researched set of university scholarship applications can still make a Korean degree affordable.

After you arrive: obligations and opportunities

One appealing feature of GKS is that it is designed to support your success rather than bind you to strict post-study conditions. Scholars complete the language year, then their degree, maintaining the academic standards required to keep the award. Achieving a strong level of Korean can bring extra benefits and greatly enriches your experience.

Beyond the funding, GKS connects you to a large network of alumni and to Korea's universities and culture, which can shape your career long after graduation. Approaching the scholarship as an opportunity to build lasting skills and relationships, not just a funded degree, helps you get the most from it.

Staying organised through the application cycle

The GKS process spans several months and many documents, so organisation is a real advantage. Build a checklist for your chosen track, note every deadline, and start gathering transcripts, certificates, and recommendation letters early. Leave time for notarisation or apostille where required, and for careful drafting and revision of your statements. Many strong candidates falter on administrative slips rather than weak profiles, so a methodical approach genuinely improves your chances.

Tips for standout application essays

The written elements of a GKS application carry enormous weight, so it is worth investing real effort in them. Your personal statement should tell a coherent story about who you are, what has shaped your goals, and why Korea is the right place to pursue them. Avoid listing achievements without meaning; instead, show how your experiences connect to a clear purpose.

Your study plan should be specific and realistic, naming the field you want to pursue and, for graduate applicants, the shape of your intended research. Be authentic rather than trying to guess what selectors want to hear, and revise your drafts several times, ideally with feedback from someone whose judgement you trust. Clear, sincere, well-structured essays are among the strongest ways to stand out in a large applicant pool.

Final thoughts

The Global Korea Scholarship is one of the most complete fully funded opportunities in international education, covering tuition, living costs, airfare, and Korean language training for both undergraduate and graduate students. Your success hinges on choosing the right track, meeting the eligibility criteria, and preparing standout statements and documents well ahead of the deadline. Start early, tailor your application to your chosen program, and studying in Korea can become a fully funded reality.

Frequently asked questions

What does the Global Korea Scholarship cover?+

GKS generally covers full tuition, a monthly living stipend, a settlement allowance, medical insurance, round-trip airfare, one year of Korean language training, and a degree completion grant. Exact amounts are set each year, so check the current guidelines.

What is the difference between the embassy and university tracks?+

In the Embassy track you apply through the Korean embassy in your country, which handles first-round selection. In the University track you apply directly to a participating Korean university that selects and recommends you. You may only use one track per cycle.

Do I need to speak Korean to apply for GKS?+

No. GKS includes a year of Korean language training, and you can apply without fluency. However, demonstrating Korean or English proficiency can strengthen your application, and a high Korean level can unlock extra benefits later.

Is GKS available for both undergraduate and graduate students?+

Yes. GKS has separate tracks for undergraduate degrees and for master's and doctoral study, each with its own guidelines and timeline, and both include a year of Korean language study before the degree begins.

When should I start preparing my GKS application?+

Begin several months before the application window, which usually opens in the first half of the year. Gathering transcripts, translating and notarising documents, writing statements, and securing recommendations all take significant time.
