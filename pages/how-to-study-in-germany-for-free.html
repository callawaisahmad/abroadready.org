How to Study in Germany for Free: Tuition-Free Universities & Scholarships

🗓️ Jun 20, 2026⏱️ 12 min read✍️ AbroadReady Team

It sounds too good to be true, but it is real: you can study in Germany for free, or very close to it, at many of the country's public universities. Germany largely abolished tuition fees at public universities for undergraduate and many master's programmes, including for international students, which means the biggest cost of higher education elsewhere can be almost zero. If your goal is a high-quality, affordable degree, learning how to study in Germany for free is one of the smartest moves you can make.

This country guide explains how tuition-free study in Germany actually works, which universities and programmes qualify, the costs you do still need to plan for, and the scholarships that can cover your living expenses. It is practical, honest, and designed to help you build a realistic plan.

Key takeaways

  • Most public universities in Germany charge no tuition for bachelor's and many master's programmes, even for international students.
  • You still pay a small semester contribution (often a few hundred euros) that usually includes a public transport ticket.
  • Living costs are the main expense; you must typically prove you can support yourself through a blocked account or equivalent.
  • Some master's programmes (especially "non-consecutive" ones) and most private universities do charge tuition.
  • Scholarships such as DAAD and others can cover living costs, making it possible to study in Germany for free overall.

How is it possible to study in Germany for free?

Germany treats higher education as a public good. Public universities are funded largely by the state, so they do not rely on tuition fees the way institutions in many other countries do. Years ago, the federal states (Bundesländer) that briefly introduced tuition fees reversed course, and today public universities generally do not charge tuition for most programmes, regardless of whether you are German or international.

This is why you can study in Germany for free in terms of tuition. It is important to be precise, though: "free" refers to tuition, not to your total cost of living. You still need money for rent, food, insurance, and other essentials.

The exception: Baden-Württemberg

One notable exception is the state of Baden-Württemberg, which reintroduced tuition fees specifically for non-EU international students at its public universities. The fees are moderate compared with many countries, but they are not zero. Elsewhere, most states remain tuition-free.

Tuition-free universities in Germany

Rather than a special list of "free universities," the rule of thumb is simple: public universities across Germany are generally tuition-free for most programmes. Germany has a large number of respected public institutions spanning research universities and universities of applied sciences (Fachhochschulen), the latter being especially strong for practical, industry-focused degrees.

Key points to remember:

  • Public universities are typically free for bachelor's and consecutive master's programmes.
  • Universities of applied sciences are also usually tuition-free and excellent for engineering, business, and technical fields.
  • Private universities in Germany do charge tuition, sometimes substantial amounts, so check the status of any institution before applying.

Which programmes are free, and which are not?

The tuition-free rule mainly applies to:

  • Bachelor's degrees at public universities.
  • Consecutive master's degrees, meaning a master's that directly follows on from a related bachelor's.

You may face fees for:

  • Non-consecutive or specialised master's programmes (for example, certain MBAs or executive master's), which often charge tuition even at public universities.
  • Programmes at private institutions.
  • Public universities in Baden-Württemberg for non-EU students, as noted above.

Before committing, always confirm the tuition status of your specific programme on the university's official website.

The costs you still need to plan for

Even when tuition is free, studying in Germany is not cost-free. Budget realistically for the following.

Semester contribution

Almost every university charges a semester contribution (Semesterbeitrag), typically a few hundred euros per semester. This is not tuition; it funds student services, administration, and, very often, a semester ticket that gives you free or discounted public transport across your region. It is one of the best-value parts of studying in Germany.

Living costs

Your biggest expense is daily life: rent, food, health insurance, study materials, and leisure. Costs vary a lot by city, with large cities like Munich being more expensive than smaller university towns. Health insurance is mandatory and must be arranged before you enrol, and it is worth comparing public and private options to find the right cover for your situation. Building a realistic monthly budget before you arrive helps you avoid surprises in your first semester.

Proof of funds and the blocked account

To get a student visa, non-EU students usually must prove they can cover their living costs for the first year, commonly through a blocked account (Sperrkonto). You deposit a set annual amount, and it is released to you in monthly instalments once you arrive. This requirement exists precisely because tuition being free does not mean living is free. Plan for it early, as arranging a blocked account and gathering the necessary documents can take several weeks, and your visa application depends on it being in place.

Scholarships that help you study in Germany for free overall

If tuition is already free, scholarships in Germany usually focus on covering living costs, and that is exactly what makes a truly cost-neutral experience possible. Options include:

  • DAAD scholarships: the German Academic Exchange Service funds many international students and researchers with monthly stipends, insurance, and sometimes travel allowances.
  • Political and religious foundation scholarships: several German foundations (often called Begabtenförderungswerke) offer scholarships to talented students, including internationals.
  • Deutschlandstipendium: a merit-based scholarship jointly funded by the government and private sponsors, awarded through participating universities.
  • University and subject-specific scholarships: individual universities and departments sometimes offer their own awards.

Combining free tuition with a living-cost scholarship is the clearest route to studying in Germany at little to no personal cost. Step-by-step: how to study in Germany for free

1. Choose a tuition-free public university and programme

Search for public universities offering your subject, and confirm that your specific programme is tuition-free. Universities of applied sciences are worth considering for practical fields.

2. Check the language of instruction

Germany offers a growing number of English-taught programmes, especially at master's level, but many bachelor's programmes are taught in German. Confirm the language requirement and prepare the relevant certificate (IELTS/TOEFL for English, or TestDaF/DSH for German).

3. Meet admission requirements

Requirements vary but typically include a recognised secondary or bachelor's qualification, proof of language ability, and sometimes subject prerequisites. Some countries' qualifications require a preparatory year (Studienkolleg) before undergraduate entry; check whether this applies to you.

4. Apply to your chosen universities

Depending on the programme, you may apply through a central platform or directly to the university. Watch deadlines carefully, as they can fall well before the semester starts.

5. Arrange finances and your visa

Set up your blocked account or alternative proof of funds, secure health insurance, and apply for your student visa if required. Start early, because visa processing can take time.

6. Apply for scholarships to cover living costs

Even though tuition is free, a scholarship can cover rent and daily expenses. Apply in parallel with your university applications so you do not miss deadlines. Our AI advisor can help you plan applications and review your documents.

Common misconceptions about studying in Germany for free

  • "Free means no costs at all." Tuition is free at most public universities, but you must budget for living costs and a semester contribution.
  • "Everything is taught in English." Many programmes, especially undergraduate ones, are in German. Always check.
  • "All universities are free." Private universities charge tuition, and Baden-Wurttemberg charges non-EU students at public universities.
  • "I do not need to prove any money." Non-EU students typically must show proof of funds, often via a blocked account, to get a visa.

English-taught versus German-taught programmes

A common question for anyone hoping to study in Germany for free is whether they can do so in English. The answer is increasingly yes, especially at master's level, where the number of English-taught programmes has grown substantially across fields like engineering, computer science, business, and the natural sciences. At bachelor's level, however, many programmes are still taught in German.

Your choice affects both your options and your daily life:

  • English-taught programmes widen access for international students and usually require an English certificate such as IELTS or TOEFL. They are common in postgraduate study.
  • German-taught programmes require proof of German proficiency, often through TestDaF or DSH, and are especially common at undergraduate level.

Even if you study in English, learning German significantly improves everyday life, part-time work options, and job prospects after graduation. It is one of the best investments you can make in your overall experience.

Choosing the right type of German university

Germany's tuition-free public system includes different kinds of institutions, and choosing the right one matters as much as choosing a tuition-free option:

  • Research universities (Universitaten): Strong in theory, research, and academic depth, ideal if you plan to pursue a PhD or a research career.
  • Universities of applied sciences (Fachhochschulen): Practical, industry-focused, and often with built-in internships, excellent for direct entry into the workforce in fields like engineering and business.

Both types are typically tuition-free at public level, so base your decision on your goals rather than cost alone. If you value hands-on learning and industry links, a university of applied sciences may serve you better than a traditional research university.

How to apply: platforms and requirements

Application routes vary by programme. Many international students apply through uni-assist, a central service that checks and forwards applications for a number of German universities, while some institutions accept applications directly. Whichever route applies, expect to submit recognised qualifications, translated and certified where required, along with language certificates and, for some courses, additional documents such as a motivation letter or CV.

Some applicants from certain school systems need to complete a Studienkolleg, a preparatory year, before starting an undergraduate degree, or pass an assessment test. Check whether your qualification is directly recognised or whether this extra step applies to you, as it affects your timeline. Working while you study

Many international students in Germany are permitted to work a limited number of hours alongside their studies, subject to visa conditions. Part-time work can help with living costs and provide valuable experience, though it should never come at the expense of your studies or your visa compliance. Roles on campus, in research assistance, or in local businesses are common. Always confirm the current rules for your visa type, since limits and conditions apply and vary.

After graduation: staying in Germany

One reason Germany is so attractive is what happens after you graduate. International graduates from German universities are often able to apply for a residence permit to seek employment related to their qualification, giving you time to find a job after completing your degree. Germany has notable demand for skilled professionals in many fields, particularly in engineering, information technology, healthcare, and the sciences, and staying to work can turn a low-cost education into a strong career launchpad. This is one of the clearest ways the value of choosing to study in Germany for free compounds over time, since your qualification opens doors in a large, stable economy. Rules change over time and depend on your circumstances, so check the current requirements, but the pathway from studying to working in Germany is a genuine and valuable one.

Is studying in Germany for free right for you?

A realistic sample budget

While exact figures depend heavily on your city and lifestyle, it helps to think in categories rather than assuming everything is covered. Your main recurring costs typically include rent (by far the largest, and much higher in cities like Munich, Frankfurt, or Hamburg than in smaller towns), food and household basics, mandatory health insurance, the semester contribution, phone and internet, study materials, and some budget for leisure and travel. Because non-EU students usually must demonstrate a set amount for the year through a blocked account, that figure is a useful benchmark for what one year of living costs looks like. Securing a living-cost scholarship, or combining part-time work within your visa limits, can close the gap and make it realistic to study in Germany for free in practice, not just in tuition.

A quick checklist to get started

  • Confirm your chosen programme is at a public university and is tuition-free.
  • Check the language of instruction and prepare the required certificate.
  • Verify whether you need a Studienkolleg or preparatory step.
  • Note application routes and deadlines, including uni-assist where relevant.
  • Plan your proof of funds and arrange health insurance early.
  • Apply for scholarships to cover living costs in parallel.

For many international students, Germany offers an unbeatable combination: globally respected universities, strong links to industry and research, a high quality of life, and tuition that is free or minimal at public institutions. With careful planning around living costs, language, and visa requirements, and by securing a scholarship to cover your day-to-day expenses, it is genuinely possible to study in Germany for free and graduate with a valuable, internationally recognised degree.

Frequently asked questions

Can international students really study in Germany for free?+

Yes, in terms of tuition. Most public universities charge no tuition for bachelor's and many master's programmes, even for international students. You still pay a small semester contribution and must budget for living costs.

Is there any state where public universities charge tuition?+

Yes. Baden-Wurttemberg reintroduced moderate tuition fees for non-EU international students at its public universities. Most other German states remain tuition-free for most programmes.

What is the semester contribution?+

It is a fee of typically a few hundred euros per semester that funds student services and administration. It is not tuition, and it often includes a semester ticket for free or discounted public transport in your region.

Do I need to prove I have money to study in Germany?+

Non-EU students usually must prove they can cover living costs for the first year, commonly through a blocked account (Sperrkonto). The deposited amount is released to you in monthly instalments after you arrive.

Which scholarships help cover living costs in Germany?+

DAAD scholarships, foundation scholarships, the Deutschlandstipendium, and university-specific awards can cover living expenses. Combined with free tuition, they make studying in Germany close to cost-neutral.
